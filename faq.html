Safari Questions

The dry season (June to October) is ideal for wildlife viewing as animals gather around water sources. The wildebeest migration in the Serengeti typically crosses the Mara River between July and September. The green season (November to May) offers lush landscapes, fewer crowds, and excellent birdwatching – plus the calving season in Ndutu (January to February) is spectacular for predator action.

Tanzania’s parks are home to the “Big Five” (lion, leopard, elephant, buffalo, rhino), plus cheetahs, giraffes, zebras, wildebeest, hippos, crocodiles, and hundreds of bird species. Each park offers something unique – Serengeti for the great migration, Ngorongoro for dense wildlife in a crater, Tarangire for large elephant herds, and Lake Manyara for tree‑climbing lions and flamingos.

  • Clothing: Neutral colors (khaki, olive, brown), lightweight long sleeves for sun protection, a warm fleece for early morning drives, and a waterproof jacket.
  • Footwear: Comfortable closed shoes or hiking boots.
  • Accessories: Wide‑brimmed hat, sunglasses, sunscreen (SPF 30+), insect repellent, binoculars, and a good camera with extra batteries.
  • Health: Personal medications, malaria prophylaxis (consult your doctor), and a basic first‑aid kit.

Trekking & Kilimanjaro

No technical climbing experience is needed. Kilimanjaro is a trekking peak, meaning anyone with good physical fitness and determination can reach the summit. We recommend training 2‑3 months beforehand – focus on cardiovascular endurance (hiking, running, cycling) and leg strength. Our guides set a slow, steady pace (“pole pole”) to maximize acclimatization and success.

We recommend the 8‑day Lemosho or 7‑day Machame routes for the highest summit success rates (over 90%). These routes allow proper acclimatization and offer stunning scenery. Marangu is the only route with hut accommodation but has a lower success rate. We’ll help you choose based on your fitness level, time, and preferences.

We provide high‑quality 4‑season tents, comfortable sleeping mats, dining tents with tables/chairs, and all cooking equipment. You need to bring your own sleeping bag (or rent from us), personal clothing, hiking boots, and a daypack. A detailed packing list is sent after booking.

Zanzibar Experiences

  • Stone Town: UNESCO World Heritage walk through history, spice markets, and Freddie Mercury’s birthplace.
  • Prison Island: Giant tortoises and snorkeling in crystal‑clear waters.
  • Nakupenda Sandbank: A day on a pristine sandbank with fresh seafood.
  • Spice Farm Tour: See, smell, and taste the spices that gave Zanzibar its nickname.
  • Sunset dhow cruise: Sail into the golden hour aboard a traditional wooden boat.

Zanzibar is a year‑round destination. The dry seasons (June – October and December – February) offer sunny, beach‑perfect weather. The long rains (March – May) bring fewer crowds and lush greenery – great for cultural tours and lower rates. The short rains (November) are usually brief afternoon showers.

Zanzibar is predominantly Muslim, so modest dress is appreciated in Stone Town and local villages. For the beach, swimwear is fine, but cover up with a kanga or light clothing when away from the sand. Women should carry a scarf to cover shoulders when entering mosques or conservative areas. Lightweight, breathable fabrics are best.

Travel Tips & Practical Info

Yellow fever vaccination is required only if arriving from a yellow‑fever endemic country. Routine vaccines (Hepatitis A, Typhoid, Tetanus) are recommended. Malaria is present in many areas; we advise taking prophylactic medication (consult your travel doctor) and using insect repellent. All our lodges provide mosquito nets.

The official currency is the Tanzanian Shilling (TZS). US dollars are widely accepted (notes must be series 2009 or newer). Credit cards are accepted at larger hotels and some tour operators, but small shops and markets are cash‑only. ATMs are available in Arusha, Moshi, and Stone Town.

Swahili and English are the national languages of Tanzania. All our guides speak fluent English, and staff at hotels and restaurants are also English‑speaking. Learning a few Swahili words (“Jambo!” for hello, “Asante” for thank you) is appreciated but not necessary.
